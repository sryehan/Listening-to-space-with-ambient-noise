Core theory — interactive

Adjust the sliders to see how the key equations behave in real time.

The Bartlett (delay-and-sum) beamformer steers to direction $\theta$ by summing sensor signals after compensating travel-time delays. For a uniform linear array with element spacing $d$ and wavenumber $k = 2\pi f/c$, the output power is

$$P(\theta) = \left|\,\sum_{i=0}^{N-1} w_i\, e^{-\,j k d\, i \sin\theta}\,\right|^2$$
Bartlett processor — incoherently averaged over the bandwidth $[f_{\min}, f_{\max}]$.

The $-3\,\text{dB}$ beamwidth (Rayleigh criterion) is approximately $\Delta\theta \approx 2\arcsin\!\bigl(0.886\,\lambda / L\bigr)$, where $L = (N-1)d$ is the array aperture. Spatial aliasing (grating lobes) appears whenever $d > \lambda/2$.

The Lobkis-Weaver relation (fluctuation-dissipation theorem applied to acoustics) states that in a perfectly diffuse field, the time derivative of the ensemble-averaged cross-correlation between two receivers recovers the causal Green's function:

$$\frac{d}{d\tau}\langle C_{ij}(\tau)\rangle \approx -G(\mathbf{r}_i,\mathbf{r}_j,\tau) + G(\mathbf{r}_j,\mathbf{r}_i,-\tau)$$
Cross-correlation computed with energy normalisation in the frequency domain: $\widetilde{C}_{ij}(\omega) = \widetilde{y}_i(\omega)\widetilde{y}_j^*(\omega)\,/\,\sqrt{E_i E_j}$.

Averaging over $N_r$ independent noise realisations improves the signal-to-noise ratio as $\sqrt{N_r}$. The travel-time peak in $G$ occurs at $\tau_0 = r_{ij}/c$.

Realisations $N_r$ 50
Sensor distance $r$ (m) 0.52 m
—

FWI minimises the $\ell^2$ distance between observed waveforms $d^{\text{obs}}$ and those modelled from the current velocity model $m$:

$$J(m) = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl\|d^{\text{obs}} - d^{\text{calc}}(m)\bigr\|^2, \qquad \frac{\partial J}{\partial m} = \sum_{\omega,s} \omega^2\,\text{Re}\!\left[u(\mathbf{x},\omega)\cdot q^*(\mathbf{x},\omega)\right]$$
The gradient is the zero-lag cross-correlation of the forward wavefield $u$ and the adjoint wavefield $q$ — computed at cost of just two forward solves per source.

A multi-scale strategy (low → high frequencies) avoids cycle-skipping. The cost function should decrease monotonically; noise raises the irreducible floor.

Velocity perturbation $\Delta c/c$ +10 %
Noise level $\sigma$ 0.05

Lab I · 30 December 2025

Acoustic Source Localization

Linear array of 14 MEMS microphones, near-field Bartlett processor, time-gating, and Eigenvector beamforming in a reverberant room.

Report PDF
Lab_I_Source_Localization.pdf

Near-field condition. With array aperture $L = (N-1)d = 13 \times 0.05 = 0.65\,\text{m}$ and centre frequency $f_c = 5\,\text{kHz}$, the Fraunhofer distance is

$$R_F = \frac{2L^2}{\lambda} = \frac{2 \times (0.65)^2}{343/5000} \approx 12.3\,\text{m}$$
Since the source sits at $R \approx 1\,\text{m} \ll R_F$, a spherical-wave point-source model is mandatory.

The point-source steering vector that replaces the plane-wave phase ramp is

$$w_i(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_i|}\,e^{-jk|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_i|}$$
Range and direction are recovered simultaneously from the 2D peak of $P(\mathbf{r}) = \mathbf{w}^H(\mathbf{r})\,\mathbf{R}\,\mathbf{w}(\mathbf{r})$.

Lab II · 16 December 2025

Passive Imaging — Scattering Cylinder

32-microphone circular array, ambient noise cross-correlation, DAS (TFM) beamforming, differential imaging, and geometry optimization.

Report PDF
Lab_II_Passive_Imaging.pdf
Upload your PDF here for inline preview.

Lobkis-Weaver theorem. For two receivers recording $y_i(t)$ and $y_j(t)$ in a perfectly diffuse field, energy-normalised cross-correlation in the frequency domain yields

$$\langle C_{ij}(\tau)\rangle = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\!\left\{\frac{\widetilde{y}_i(\omega)\,\widetilde{y}_j^*(\omega)}{\sqrt{E_i E_j}}\right\}$$
Taking the time derivative recovers the causal Green's function: $\;\partial_\tau\langle C_{ij}\rangle \approx -G(\mathbf{r}_i,\mathbf{r}_j,\tau) + G(\mathbf{r}_j,\mathbf{r}_i,-\tau)$.

The DAS image at grid point $(x,y)$ sums the Green's function sampled at the two-way travel time $\tau_{ij} = (d_i + d_j)/c$ across all sensor pairs:

$$I(x,y) = \sum_{i=1}^{M}\sum_{j=1}^{M} \left|G\!\left(\mathbf{r}_i,\mathbf{r}_j,\,\frac{d_i+d_j}{c}\right)\right|$$
Differential image: $I_{\text{diff}} = |I_{\text{cyl}} - I_{\text{empty}}|$ — removes static direct-path contributions between microphone pairs.

Lab III · 2025

Full Waveform Inversion

Adjoint-state gradient, Helmholtz 2D forward solver, sensitivity kernels, multi-scale strategy, and noise robustness analysis.

Report PDF
Lab_III_FWI_Adjoint.pdf
Upload your PDF here for inline preview.

Misfit functional. FWI minimises the $\ell^2$ norm of the data residual over all sources $s$ and frequencies $\omega$:

$$J(m) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{s}\bigl\|d_s^{\text{obs}} - d_s^{\text{calc}}(m)\bigr\|^2$$
$m = 1/c^2$ is the squared slowness. The Helmholtz equation $(\nabla^2 + \omega^2 m)\,u = b$ governs the forward wavefield $u$.

Adjoint-state gradient. The gradient is the zero-lag cross-correlation of forward and adjoint wavefields — computed at the cost of just two additional forward solves per source:

$$\frac{\partial J}{\partial m}\bigg|_\mathbf{x} = \sum_{\omega,s} \omega^2\,\operatorname{Re}\!\bigl[u_s(\mathbf{x},\omega)\cdot q_s^*(\mathbf{x},\omega)\bigr]$$
The adjoint source is the data residual $b_q = \mathbf{E}_{\text{rec}}\bigl(u|_{\text{rec}} - d^{\text{obs}}\bigr)$; the adjoint wavefield $q$ is solved with the transposed system $\mathbf{A}^T q = b_q$.
